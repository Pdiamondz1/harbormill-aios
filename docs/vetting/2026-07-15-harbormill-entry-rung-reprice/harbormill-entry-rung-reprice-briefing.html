A five-lens synthesis: practitioner, academic, skeptic, economist, and historian. Every claim independently checked against its primary source before publication. Most of them did not survive intact.

01

The 60-Second Summary

The settled facts are official and they are bleak: US small-business AI adoption sits near 18% and, per the Census Bureau, did not change significantly among firms with fewer than 20 employees while larger firms pulled away to 37% — adoption is diverging away from the SMB segment. More than half of firms expect to spend no more than $200 per employee on AI in 2026 (Atlanta Fed); Ramp's card data puts the median at $11.38 per employee per month. A 40-person business therefore has an entire annual AI budget somewhere between roughly $1k and $8k — meaning a $10,000 build is not a line item in their AI budget; it is several multiples of the whole thing. The contested interpretation is what follows from that. One reading says price low to buy proof; another says the AI budget is simply the wrong pocket, and the work must be priced against a labour line — the part-time hire not made — where $2.5k/mo reads as cheap rather than 200x over. What collapsed under verification is the entire pricing-advice literature that motivates repricing at all: the "productize and raise your fees" evidence base traces to a vendor selling coaching to consultants, with no disclosed methodology and no independent corroboration anywhere. The price-as-quality signal is real but small (r = .286) and weakest for services. The outcome guarantee fails as a signal for a solo, and inverts adverse selection onto the seller. The one instrument that survives all five lenses and the peer-reviewed evidence is the small paid diagnostic — and it works by screening, not by commitment.

The SMB "AI budget" is an order of magnitude smaller than the pitch assumes — and the widely-cited $18k figure is fabricated

9/10

Price does not reliably signal quality — and the effect is weakest precisely for services sold by unknowns
3
Reliability: Medium-high — peer-reviewed meta-analysis
7/10

The folk theory that a higher price makes an unknown seller look more credible is the load-bearing assumption behind almost every "raise your rates" argument. The meta-analytic evidence does not support it at useful strength. Völckner & Hofmann (2007, Marketing Letters), pooling 71 effects, put the price↔perceived-quality correlation at r = .286 — roughly 9% of variance — and report it is significantly weaker for services, weaker for familiar buyers, and declining over time. The related claim that a published hourly rate anchors a differently-denominated project price is an extrapolation: anchoring itself is real and large (Petrowsky et al. 2025, OBHDP: 374 effects, N=16,334; first-offer↔final-agreement r = 0.62), but anchors are demonstrated within a single issue, and no study shows a rate anchoring a project price. Anchoring also attenuates with professional experience — i.e. in experienced buyers. Score capped at 7 because these two meta-analyses were surfaced by the Academic lens but were not independently re-verified by this run's verification agents; the citations are checkable but were not checked here.

A paid diagnostic works — but by screening, not by commitment. The mechanism everyone cites is the wrong one
4
Reliability: Medium-high — field RCT, but transfer to consulting untested
7/10

Consultants justify paid discovery with sunk cost: "if they pay, they're invested, so they follow through." That mechanism is rejected. Arkes & Blumer (1985), the canonical study, was n=54 and significant in the first half of the season only; it has never been replicated for this purpose. Foot-in-the-door (Freedman & Fraser 1966; meta-analytic r ≈ .17) actually argues for a free first ask, since its initial request is free and tiny. The decisive evidence is a field RCT designed precisely to separate the two mechanisms — Ashraf, Berry & Shapiro (2010, American Economic Review) — which finds "economically important screening effects... no consistent evidence of sunk-cost effects." The practical consequence is a reversal of purpose: a paid audit's price should be set to filter serious buyers, not to earn revenue or to manufacture commitment. Non-zero is what matters; large is not. This also means raising a screening price past the point where it screens buys nothing and costs volume.

MIT NANDA's "95% of AI pilots fail"

The most quoted number in enterprise AI, and it does not hold up — but the debunking does not help you. The primary report is not peer-reviewed, self-labels as "Preliminary Findings," and lists a co-author as its sole reviewer. Its actual claim is "95% of organizations are getting zero return," not "95% of pilots fail" — two different propositions fused by the press. The famous "150 interviews" appears nowhere in the primary (it reports 52 organizational interviews and 153 senior-leader surveys); it is a press artifact. The report concedes its figures are "directionally accurate based on individual interviews rather than official company reporting." The strongest critique (Raynovich, Futuriom) notes the 95% is asserted in a single sentence with no supporting data and appears back-derived; Wharton's Kevin Werbach says MIT should release the data "or it should retract the report." The claim that NANDA sells a "$250k membership" is commentary, not fact: NANDA is an MIT Media Lab group founded by a co-author and Media Lab corporate membership runs ≥$250k/yr, but no primary source prices a NANDA membership. Why it still matters: the headline already reached your buyers. You inherit the skepticism without the truth.

03

The Hidden Connection

Two findings look like they cancel out. The Skeptic shows that incumbents are absorbing exactly the workflows an automation consultant sells — Intuit Assist already does AP/AR, HubSpot bundles agents into plans SMBs already pay for — so any build has a replacement cost falling toward zero on someone else's roadmap. The Historian shows the opposite survived: in the RPA wave, boutiques selling "bots" were crushed between the Big Four and the vendors, while those selling "AP invoice cycle time" lived. Same workflow. Opposite outcomes.

They are not in conflict. They are the same workflow described at two altitudes, and the altitude is the entire business. A vendor can ship the feature; a vendor cannot be accountable for your number. And this is where the fifth lens closes the loop: the Academic's reason an outcome guarantee is uninsurable is that nobody can measure AI's revenue effect — the Federal Reserve itself declines to. Which means the measurement instrument is not the paperwork before the product. It is the product. The paid audit that establishes the baseline is simultaneously the screen (per the Ashraf RCT), the only credible signal available to an unknown, and the sole thing that could ever make an outcome-based deal underwritable later.

The rung you are tempted to give away or raise — the audit — is the only durable asset on the ladder. The rung you are tempted to charge more for — the build — is what Intuit ships free next quarter.

The assumption this briefing rests on (and the missing 6th lens)

All five lenses priced services — a person's time, packaged. None modelled a software product with clone-per-client economics, which is the asset actually in hand. The missing sixth lens is the software economist, and it could invert the arithmetic: if the marginal cost of the tenth deployment approaches zero, then "the SMB AI budget is only $8k/year" stops binding, because the offer is no longer competing for the tooling line at $200/employee — it is competing with a $4,500/month hire not made. Every price in this briefing is derived from a services frame that may be the wrong frame entirely.

The omission that could invert the findings outright is churn. No lens modelled retention. A $3–10k/month retainer sold to a firm whose entire annual AI spend is $1k–$8k is definitionally not an AI purchase — it is labour substitution, and it will be re-evaluated against a salary every year. If that retainer churns at six months, the ladder is not a ladder; it is a treadmill, and every finding above is optimising the wrong loop.

04

What To Actually Do Differently

For a solo operator with real expertise, no public audience, no published case studies, and an un-activated warm network. These are moves, not principles.

01
Stop repricing. The price card is not the constraint and never was.

Every lens here, and every persona in the preceding adversarial roast — including the 9/10 bull — converged on distribution. A price with zero transactions is unfalsifiable, which is exactly why it is comfortable to debate. Tuning it feels like progress and produces none.

02
Price against the labour line, never the AI line. Delete the phrase "AI budget" from every conversation.

Finding 1 is the whole argument: more than half of firms expect to spend ≤$200/employee. Against a tooling budget a $2,500/month retainer is 200x over and the conversation is dead. Against a $4,500/month part-time hire it is cheap. Same number, different pocket — and the second pocket is 20x deeper.

03
Keep the audit paid and small. Set its price to screen, not to earn.

Per Finding 4, the mechanism is selection, not commitment — so the price only needs to be non-zero enough to filter tyre-kickers. Raising it toward $5k buys nothing the screen already provides and costs volume you cannot spare. A paid audit also preserves the right to say "AI is the wrong answer here," which a free audit financially forbids.

04
Do not offer an outcome guarantee. It fails three ways, independently.

Moorthy & Srinivasan's model requires the seller's transaction cost to be mid-range; for a solo a refund forfeits 100% of sunk labour. It also presupposes an experience good — consulting is a credence good, where quality is unverifiable even after delivery. And it inverts adverse selection onto you: the clients who self-select into a guarantee are those with the worst-instrumented processes, i.e. the ones most likely to dispute. The practitioner's version is blunter — the argument is never "did it work," it is "did you use it," and you cannot invoice "your receptionist ignored it."

05
Sell one vertical's number, not the automation.

The RPA precedent is exact: "bots" got commoditized, "AP invoice cycle time" survived. Intuit can ship an AR feature; it cannot own a named customer's DSO. Note the historian's warning on the other half of the strategy — training is bait, not business. The vendor always gives training away eventually (HubSpot Academy killed paid social-media training; Salesforce and HubSpot are thinning their partner programs right now, effective July–August 2026).

06
Do not hire, and do not scale headcount into this boom.

Viant's revenue fell 73% in one year ($127.2M → $34.6M, audited). Scient's stock peaked roughly six months before its revenue did — the market repriced first. Those firms died because they had a bench. A solo taking the same cut has a slow quarter. Structural uncollapsibility is the solo's only real edge over funded competitors; spending it on headcount is the one unforced error available.

05

The Frontier Question

When the vendors give away both the training and the automation — which every prior cycle says happens within about 36 months, and which Salesforce and HubSpot are executing right now — what is a small business still willing to pay a human for?

No lens addressed this, because each was asked what the market pays today. But the historian's pattern and the skeptic's roadmap point at the same date. HubSpot terminates its Provider Program in August 2026 and imposes a $400/month membership; Salesforce collapsed four partner tiers into two. The generalist implementation partner is being deliberately thinned out of existence, on a published schedule.

If the answer is "nothing," the entire category is a bridge trade and the correct play is to maximize cash now and exit. If the answer is "accountability for a number" — the thing a vendor structurally cannot sell, because Intuit cannot be liable for your DSO — then the durable asset was never the automation or the teaching. It is the measurement instrument and the person who signs their name under the result. That reading is consistent with every surviving finding here. But no lens tested whether an SMB will actually buy accountability from a solo operator with no bench, no brand, and no insurer standing behind them — and that, not the price card, is the question the business rests on.
